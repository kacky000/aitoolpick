Obsidian has become a go-to tool for knowledge workers, researchers, and writers who need a powerful local-first note-taking solution. However, it's not the right fit for everyone. Whether you're looking for better collaboration features, a simpler interface, or cloud-based synchronization, there are excellent alternatives worth considering in 2026.

This guide compares 7 outstanding Obsidian alternatives to help you find the perfect note-taking app for your workflow.

## 1. Notion — All-in-One Workspace

Notion has evolved into far more than a note-taking app. It's a complete workspace that combines notes, databases, wikis, and project management in one cloud-based platform.

**Key Strengths:**
- Powerful databases with filtering, sorting, and relations
- Seamless team collaboration with real-time editing
- Templates library for faster setup
- Beautiful UI with drag-and-drop blocks
- Web-based, accessible from any device
- Strong community and integration ecosystem

**Pricing:** Free tier available; Pro plan at $10/month per user

**Best For:** Teams, project-focused note-takers, and anyone who wants collaboration built-in

Unlike Obsidian's local-first approach, Notion stores everything in the cloud, making it ideal for teams that need to share and collaborate on notes simultaneously. If you're already invested in the Notion ecosystem, this is a natural choice.

## 2. Logseq — Open-Source Outlining

Logseq shares Obsidian's philosophy of local-first, open-source note-taking, but uses an outliner structure instead of traditional files. It also pioneered the daily notes workflow that Obsidian later adopted.

**Key Strengths:**
- Open-source and community-driven
- Excellent outliner-based organization
- Powerful graph visualization
- Local-first with optional cloud sync
- Free and feature-complete
- Strong focus on knowledge building through backlinking

**Pricing:** Free (open-source)

**Best For:** Users who prefer outliner-style notes and value open-source software

If you love Obsidian's philosophy but want a different organizational structure, Logseq is a compelling alternative. It handles complex note relationships with its graph view and bidirectional linking just as well as Obsidian.

## 3. Roam Research — The Original Bidirectional Linker

Roam Research is the pioneer that introduced bidirectional linking to mainstream note-taking. It popularized the "thinking tool" category that Obsidian later dominated.

**Key Strengths:**
- Created the bidirectional linking standard
- Daily notes workflow built into the core
- Powerful block-level backlinking
- Web-based with cloud sync
- Active research community
- Strong API for extensions

**Pricing:** $15/month

**Best For:** Researchers, academics, and users building networked knowledge systems

Roam is pricier than Obsidian, but it's specifically designed for people who think in interconnected ideas. If networked note-taking is your primary workflow, Roam's cloud-based approach eliminates sync concerns.

## 4. Apple Notes — Simple and Integrated

Apple Notes often gets overlooked, but it has matured significantly. With iOS 17 and macOS Sonoma improvements, it's now a serious contender for simple note-taking.

**Key Strengths:**
- Free for all Apple users
- Seamless syncing across iPhone, iPad, Mac, and iCloud Web
- Folder organization and smart folders
- Mentions and @notifications for collaboration
- New collaborative features in 2026
- Excellent search functionality
- Zero learning curve for Apple ecosystem users

**Pricing:** Free

**Best For:** Apple-exclusive users who value simplicity and tight OS integration

While Apple Notes lacks advanced features like backlinking and graph visualization, its new collaboration features and improved organization make it viable for teams within the Apple ecosystem.

## 5. Craft — Beautiful Native Note-Taking

Craft focuses on elegant design and a delightful user experience across Mac and iOS. It strikes a balance between simplicity and powerful features.

**Key Strengths:**
- Stunning native interface on Mac and iOS
- Document-focused with folders and projects
- Real-time collaboration
- Markdown support
- Synced across Apple devices
- Growing integration ecosystem
- Free tier with premium options

**Pricing:** Free with optional premium features

**Best For:** Mac/iOS users who prioritize aesthetics and seamless native experience

Craft doesn't have Obsidian's local-first philosophy or backlinking, but its focus on beautiful design and native performance appeals to users who spend significant time in note-taking apps.

## 6. Capacities — Object-Based Note-Taking

Capacities represents a newer approach to digital notes. Instead of organizing by folders and backlinks, it uses objects and database relationships to structure knowledge.

**Key Strengths:**
- Modern object-based architecture
- Built-in templates for different content types
- Powerful object relationships
- Clean, minimal interface
- Cloud-based with offline support
- Strong privacy focus
- Growing feature set in 2026

**Pricing:** Freemium model with paid tiers

**Best For:** Users who want a modern alternative to folder-based organization

Capacities offers a fresh perspective on how to organize notes. Instead of thinking in terms of documents and folders, you think in objects and their relationships. This can be more intuitive for some workflows than traditional note hierarchies.

## 7. Joplin — Open-Source Local-First

Joplin is another open-source alternative that prioritizes local file storage and user privacy. It's the closest spiritual successor to Obsidian in this list.

**Key Strengths:**
- Open-source and completely free
- True local-first storage
- Markdown support with rich editing
- Cross-platform (Windows, Mac, Linux, iOS, Android)
- Encrypted cloud sync available
- Active community development
- No vendor lock-in

**Pricing:** Free (donations welcome)

**Best For:** Privacy-conscious users and those who want complete control over their data

Like Obsidian, Joplin stores notes locally and offers optional encrypted sync. It lacks some of Obsidian's advanced features like graph visualization, but it's a solid, privacy-respecting choice for those wary of cloud storage.

## How to Choose Your Alternative

**Choose Notion if:** You need collaboration, databases, and a complete workspace in one platform.

**Choose Logseq if:** You love outliner-style organization and want open-source software.

**Choose Roam if:** Networked thinking and research are central to your workflow.

**Choose Apple Notes if:** You're fully in the Apple ecosystem and value simplicity.

**Choose Craft if:** Design matters as much as functionality.

**Choose Capacities if:** You want a modern, object-based alternative to traditional folders.

**Choose Joplin if:** Privacy and local-first storage are non-negotiable.

## Conclusion

Obsidian remains an excellent choice for solo, knowledge-focused users who want powerful backlinking and complete control over their data. But it's not the only option. Each alternative here excels in different areas—collaboration, design, privacy, or organizational philosophy.

The best note-taking app is the one you'll actually use consistently. Consider your primary workflow: Are you collaborating with others? Do you need mobile access? Is privacy paramount? Do you want open-source software? Your answers to these questions will guide you to the right alternative.

Test a few of these options in your workflow. Most offer free trials or free tiers, so you can experience them before committing.
