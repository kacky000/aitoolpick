# Make (formerly Integromat) Review 2026: Is It Worth It?

Make — formerly known as Integromat — is one of the most powerful automation platforms available in 2026. With a visual scenario builder, support for over 1,000 apps, and a free tier that punches well above its weight, Make has become the go-to automation tool for power users who find Zapier too simple (or too expensive). This review covers everything you need to know.

## What Is Make?

Make is a visual workflow automation platform. You build "scenarios" — automated sequences that connect apps and services to move data and trigger actions without code. Think: when a new form is submitted on your website, automatically create a row in Google Sheets, send a Slack notification, and add the contact to your email list.

What separates Make from basic automation tools is its depth. You can build complex multi-path scenarios with conditional logic, loops, error handling, and data transformation — things that would require custom code in simpler tools.

## Visual Scenario Builder: Make's Killer Feature

Make's drag-and-drop scenario builder is the best visual automation interface available. Each "module" (app integration) appears as a bubble on a canvas. You connect them with lines, add routers for branching logic, and can visually trace exactly what happens with your data at each step.

This visual approach makes complex automation understandable. Even developers who could write this logic in code often prefer Make's builder because they can share and explain the automation to non-technical stakeholders.

Key features of the builder:
- **Routers**: Split your automation into multiple paths based on conditions
- **Iterators and aggregators**: Process arrays of data item by item, then combine results
- **Error handlers**: Define what happens when a module fails — retry, ignore, or route to a different path
- **Variables and functions**: Transform data with built-in functions (date formatting, text manipulation, math)
- **Webhooks**: Trigger scenarios from any external event in real time

For apps not on the list, Make's HTTP module lets you connect to any REST API, and its webhook support lets you receive data from virtually any source.

## Pricing Deep Dive

### Free Plan
The free plan gives you 1,000 operations per month and 2 active scenarios. Operations are roughly equivalent to "tasks" in Zapier's terminology — each module execution counts as one operation.

1,000 operations sounds limited, but for simple automations (a form submission triggers 3-4 module runs), you can handle 250+ triggers per month for free. For experimentation and learning, the free plan is genuinely useful.

### Core ($10.59/month)
The jump to Core gives you 10,000 operations and 3 active scenarios — still relatively limited in active scenarios. For most solo users with a few regular automations, Core is sufficient.

### Pro ($18.82/month)
Pro removes the active scenario limit, adds scheduling at 1-minute intervals (vs. 15-minute minimum on Core), and includes full execution history logging. This is the sweet spot for freelancers and small businesses with multiple active automations.

### Teams ($34.12/month)
Teams adds multi-user collaboration, shared scenario ownership, and team management features. For agencies or small teams where multiple people manage automations, Teams is worth the premium.

## Make vs Zapier: How Do They Compare?

Make and Zapier are direct competitors, but they target different users.

**Zapier** is easier. Its linear "trigger → action" model is intuitive for beginners, and its 6,000+ app integrations beat Make's 1,000+. Zapier's free plan offers 100 tasks/month. Paid plans start at $19.99/month for 750 tasks.

**Make** is more powerful. Once you move beyond simple two-step automations, Make's visual builder, multi-path routing, and data transformation capabilities make it dramatically more capable. Make is also significantly cheaper for the same functionality — 10,000 operations at $10.59/month vs. Zapier's $49/month for 2,000 tasks.

The cost difference is substantial at scale. An agency running complex automations could easily spend $200-500/month on Zapier for the same workflows that cost $34-50/month on Make.

**Choose Zapier if**: You need maximum app coverage, you're not technical, and you want simplicity above all else.
**Choose Make if**: You need complex logic, you're cost-conscious, or you're comfortable with a steeper learning curve.

## What Make Does Well

**Visual clarity**: The canvas-based builder is the best in class. Debugging an automation is much easier when you can see the entire flow.

**Data handling**: Make's built-in functions for transforming data (parsing JSON, formatting dates, manipulating text) are more powerful than Zapier's equivalent.

**Error handling**: Make lets you define explicit error handling paths. When a Zapier zap fails, you get an email. When a Make scenario fails, you can route it to a backup workflow.

**Webhooks**: Make's webhook support is fast, reliable, and works on all paid plans. Real-time triggering is available from $10.59/month.

**Value**: 10,000 operations for $10.59/month is exceptional value for small businesses and freelancers.

## What Make Does Poorly

**Learning curve**: Make is not beginner-friendly. The concepts (routers, iterators, aggregators, bundles) require a few hours of learning before you're productive.

**App coverage**: 1,000+ apps sounds like a lot, but Zapier's 6,000+ means you're more likely to find a native integration for niche tools on Zapier.

**Support**: Make's documentation has improved, but Zapier's community and help resources are still more extensive.

**Interface speed**: The visual builder can feel slow on complex scenarios with many modules, especially on older hardware.

## Who Should Use Make?

Make is the right choice if you:
- Run a business or agency with complex automation needs
- Are comfortable spending a few hours learning a tool to unlock significant power
- Want to automate multi-step, branching workflows that Zapier handles poorly
- Are looking for Zapier-level functionality at a significantly lower price
- Work with data transformation, API calls, or webhook-based integrations

Make is not ideal if you:
- Are completely new to automation and want something simple
- Need integrations with very niche or obscure apps
- Want a "set and forget" tool that requires zero technical thinking

## Our Verdict

Make is one of the best values in the automation software market. The visual builder is genuinely impressive, the free tier is useful, and the paid plans are significantly cheaper than Zapier for equivalent functionality.

The learning curve is real — plan to spend 2-3 hours with Make's tutorials before you're fully comfortable. But that investment pays off quickly for anyone running more than a handful of automations.

**Rating: 4.5/5**
Best for power users and growing businesses. Beginners may prefer starting with Zapier's simplicity.
