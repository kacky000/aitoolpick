Looking for the best note taking apps? We tested and compared the top options available in 2026, evaluating features, pricing, ease of use, and real-world performance.

The right note-taking app can transform how you think, learn, and work. But with dozens of options available in 2026, finding the best fit for your workflow can be overwhelming. We've tested every major note-taking app extensively — here are the 7 best, with honest assessments of each.

## Quick Recommendations

- **Best all-around**: [Notion](/tools/notion/)
- **Best for privacy & power users**: [Obsidian](/tools/obsidian/)
- **Best for quick capture**: Google Keep
- **Best for Apple users**: [Apple Notes](/tools/apple-notes/) / Bear
- **Best for researchers**: [Logseq](/tools/logseq/)
- **Best legacy option**: [Evernote](/tools/evernote/)

## 1. Notion — Best All-Around Note-Taking App

**Pricing**: Free / Plus $10/mo / Business $18/mo / Enterprise (custom)

Notion isn't just a note-taking app — it's a full workspace that combines notes, databases, wikis, and project management. For a deep dive into pricing, see our [Notion pricing breakdown](/blog/notion-pricing-2026).

**Strengths:**
- Incredibly flexible — databases, templates, wikis, and notes in one tool
- AI features built in (summarization, writing assistance, autofill)
- Excellent collaboration for teams
- Massive template ecosystem
- API for automations

**Weaknesses:**
- Can feel slow with very large workspaces
- Offline support has improved but still not perfect
- Learning curve for advanced features
- All data stored in the cloud (no local-first option)

**Best for**: Students, teams, knowledge workers, and anyone who wants one tool for everything. If you're considering alternatives, check our [best Notion alternatives guide](/blog/best-notion-alternatives-2026).

## 2. Obsidian — Best for Privacy & Power Users

**Pricing**: Free (personal) / Commercial $50/user/year / Sync $4/mo / Publish $8/mo

Obsidian stores everything as local Markdown files on your device. No cloud dependency, no vendor lock-in. Your notes are yours forever.

**Strengths:**
- Local-first — your files, your device, your control
- Backlinks and knowledge graph for connected thinking
- Incredible plugin ecosystem (1,000+ community plugins)
- Lightning fast, even with thousands of notes
- Works offline by default

**Weaknesses:**
- No real-time collaboration
- Sync requires paid add-on or DIY setup
- Mobile apps are functional but not as polished
- Steeper learning curve for non-technical users

**Best for**: Researchers, developers, privacy-conscious users, and anyone building a personal knowledge base.

## 3. Evernote — The Legacy Option That's Fighting Back

**Pricing**: Free (limited) / Personal $14.99/mo / Professional $17.99/mo

Evernote was the original note-taking app, and after years of stagnation, it's been reinventing itself under Bending Spoons' ownership. The app is faster, cleaner, and has added AI features.

**Strengths:**
- Excellent web clipper (still the best in class)
- Strong OCR — search text inside images and PDFs
- Mature, polished interface
- Good email-to-note functionality
- AI-powered search and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- Free plan is severely limited (1 device, 60 MB uploads)
- Expensive compared to alternatives
- Has lost trust with long-time users due to past changes
- Feature set hasn't kept pace with Notion or Obsidian

**Best for**: Users who rely heavily on web clipping and scanning physical documents.

## 4. Apple Notes — Best Free Option for Apple Users

**Pricing**: Free (included with Apple devices)

Apple Notes has quietly become a surprisingly capable note-taking app. With iOS/iPadOS 19 and macOS updates, it now supports tags, smart folders, collaboration, and even basic scanning.

**Strengths:**
- Completely free with no tier limitations
- Seamless sync across iPhone, iPad, Mac
- Fast and reliable
- Handwriting support with Apple Pencil
- Built-in document scanning
- Quick Notes feature on iPadOS and macOS

**Weaknesses:**
- No Windows or Android apps
- Limited formatting and organization
- No backlinks or knowledge graph
- Basic collaboration compared to Notion
- No plugin or extension system

**Best for**: Apple ecosystem users who want a simple, reliable, free solution.

## 5. Google Keep — Best for Quick Capture

**Pricing**: Free

Google Keep isn't trying to be a full note-taking system. It's a fast, lightweight tool for capturing ideas, creating checklists, and setting reminders. Think of it as digital sticky notes.

**Strengths:**
- Extremely fast — open the app and start typing
- Color-coded notes for visual organization
- Location-based and time-based reminders
- Excellent Google Workspace integration
- Works on every platform (web, Android, iOS)
- Voice notes with automatic transcription

**Weaknesses:**
- Very basic formatting (no Markdown, no rich text)
- No folder structure — labels only
- Not suitable for long-form notes
- Limited organization for large collections
- No offline editing on web

**Best for**: Quick thoughts, shopping lists, reminders, and anyone who needs to capture ideas [instantly](/tools/instantly/).

## 6. Bear — Best Writing Experience on Apple

**Pricing**: Free (basic) / [Bear](/tools/bear-notes/) Pro $2.99/mo or $29.99/year

Bear is a beautiful, Markdown-based note-taking app exclusive to Apple platforms. If you care about the writing experience, Bear is hard to beat.

**Strengths:**
- Gorgeous, distraction-free writing interface
- Native Markdown with inline previews
- Fast and lightweight
- Excellent tagging system with nested tags
- Export to multiple formats (PDF, HTML, DOCX, Markdown)
- Focus mode for distraction-free writing

**Weaknesses:**
- Apple only (no Windows, Android, or web app)
- No collaboration features
- No databases or advanced organization
- Limited media embedding

**Best for**: Writers, bloggers, and Apple users who want a beautiful Markdown editor.

## 7. Logseq — Best for Researchers & Connected Thinking

**Pricing**: Free (open source) / Logseq Sync $5/mo

Logseq is an open-source, privacy-first outliner with powerful bidirectional linking. It's similar to Obsidian in philosophy but uses an outliner format instead of long-form documents.

**Strengths:**
- Open source and local-first
- Powerful outliner with block-level references
- Knowledge graph visualization
- PDF annotation built in
- Flashcard system (spaced repetition) built in
- Active community and plugin ecosystem

**Weaknesses:**
- Outliner format isn't for everyone
- Mobile apps are still maturing
- Steeper learning curve than mainstream apps
- Smaller ecosystem than Obsidian

**Best for**: Researchers, academics, and Zettelkasten enthusiasts who prefer outlining over long-form writing.

## How to Choose: Use Case Recommendations

### For Students
**Top pick: Notion** — The free plan is generous for education (free Plus for students), and the flexibility to create study dashboards, class notes, and project trackers is unmatched.

### For Researchers & Academics
**Top pick: Logseq or Obsidian** — Bidirectional linking and knowledge graphs are essential for connecting ideas across papers and topics.

### For Creative Writers
**Top pick: Bear** — The writing experience is beautiful, and Markdown support makes it easy to export to any format.

### For Teams
**Top pick: Notion** — Real-time collaboration, shared wikis, and database views [make](/tools/make/) it the clear winner for team knowledge management.

### For Privacy-First Users
**Top pick: Obsidian** — Local files, no cloud dependency, and end-to-end encrypted sync (paid add-on).

## Our Final Recommendation

If you want one note-taking app that does everything, **Notion is the best choice in 2026**. It's the most versatile tool on this list, and the AI features [make](/tools/make/) it even more powerful.

If privacy and data ownership matter most, **Obsidian is the way to go**. Your notes stay on your device, in plain Markdown files that will be readable decades from now.
