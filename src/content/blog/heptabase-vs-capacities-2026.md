**Heptabase** and **Capacities** are both designed for people who find traditional note-taking apps too flat. They want spatial, visual, or structured ways to organize knowledge. But the two tools take fundamentally different approaches: Heptabase builds on whiteboards, Capacities builds on typed objects. That distinction shapes everything else.

## What Is Heptabase?

Heptabase is a visual knowledge management tool centered around **whiteboards** (called "maps"). You create cards containing notes, then arrange them spatially on infinite canvases. The core idea is that physical placement — clustering related cards, drawing connections, creating visual hierarchies — helps you understand complex topics in ways that linear documents cannot.

For a full breakdown, check our [Heptabase review](/blog/heptabase-review-2026).

## What Is Capacities?

Capacities is a knowledge management tool built around **typed objects**. Instead of generic pages or notes, everything you create has a type — Book, Person, Meeting, Project, Idea — and each type has its own properties and relationships. Think of it as a personal CRM for your thoughts, where structure is enforced by design rather than by discipline.

## How They Organize Knowledge

### Heptabase: Whiteboards and Spatial Thinking

Heptabase's core unit is the **card**. Cards are rich-text documents that can contain text, images, code blocks, and embeds. But cards don't live in folders — they live on **whiteboards**. You drag cards onto a whiteboard, position them spatially, draw arrows between them, and group them into sections.

This works well for breaking down research, planning projects visually, studying complex subjects, and connecting ideas across domains. Cards can appear on multiple whiteboards without duplication — the same card shows up wherever it's relevant.

### Capacities: Objects and Structured Relationships

Capacities organizes everything as **typed objects**. When you create something, you choose its type: Book, Person, Tweet, Project, Tag, or a custom type you define. Each type has specific properties — a Book has Author, Rating, Date Read; a Person has Company, Role, Last Contact.

The power comes from **relationships between objects**. Link a Person to a Meeting, link that Meeting to a Project, link that Project to several Ideas. Capacities automatically shows you these connections in each object's sidebar. This is ideal for anyone who thinks in categories and wants their notes pre-organized with consistent structure.

### Backlinks

Both tools support backlinks, but the implementation differs. Heptabase shows backlinks at the bottom of each card — standard bidirectional linking. Capacities treats backlinks as first-class relationships between objects. When you mention a Person in a Meeting note, that Person's page automatically shows the Meeting in its "Mentioned in" section. Capacities' approach is more structured; Heptabase's is more freeform.

### Daily Notes

Heptabase offers a journal feature for daily notes. You can drag any section from a journal entry onto a whiteboard as a card, which bridges daily capture with long-term knowledge organization.

Capacities has daily notes too, and they integrate with the object system. Mention a Book or Person in your daily note, and those objects automatically link back. Both implementations are solid, but Capacities' automatic linking from daily notes feels more seamless.

### AI Features

Heptabase uses AI for search and summarization across your knowledge base. You can ask questions about your notes, and it surfaces relevant cards.

Capacities offers AI chat that can reference your objects, plus auto-tagging suggestions and smart link recommendations. The AI feels more deeply integrated into the object system, suggesting connections you might not have made manually.

Heptabase has a single paid plan at $9.99/month (or $7.99/month billed annually). No free tier — just a 7-day trial. You get full access to everything.

Capacities offers a free tier with basic object types and limited storage. The paid Believer plan at $12/month unlocks custom object types, more storage, advanced AI, and priority support.

For cost-conscious users, Capacities' free tier is a meaningful advantage. You can use it indefinitely and only upgrade when you need custom types or more AI features. Heptabase requires payment from day one.

## Who Should Choose Heptabase?

Heptabase is the better choice if you **think spatially**. If you find yourself sketching diagrams on paper, arranging sticky notes on walls, or wishing you could "see" how your ideas connect on a canvas, Heptabase's whiteboard approach will feel natural.

Specific use cases where Heptabase excels:
- **Students and researchers** breaking down complex subjects into visual maps
- **Writers** organizing book outlines or article structures spatially
- **Product managers** mapping out feature relationships and dependencies
- **Anyone migrating from tools like Miro or FigJam** for knowledge work

If you've tried Obsidian and liked the linking but wished for a more visual interface, see our [Obsidian vs Capacities comparison](/blog/obsidian-vs-capacities-2026) for another angle on this.

## Who Should Choose Capacities?

Capacities is the better choice if you **think in categories and relationships**. If you naturally want to classify information — this is a Book, that's a Person, this belongs to that Project — Capacities' object system will feel like it was made for you.

Specific use cases where Capacities excels:
- **CRM-style knowledge management** — tracking people, companies, and interactions
- **Book readers and content consumers** who want structured logs of what they've read, watched, and learned
- **Professionals** managing clients, projects, and meeting notes in one connected system
- **Anyone who found Notion too unstructured** — Capacities enforces consistency through types

## The Bottom Line

Heptabase and Capacities solve the same problem — making knowledge visual and connected — through opposite approaches. Heptabase gives you a blank canvas and lets spatial arrangement create structure. Capacities gives you a type system and lets object relationships create structure.

Try this test: when you encounter a new idea, do you want to place it on a visual map next to related concepts? Choose Heptabase. Do you want to classify it as a type and link it to other objects? Choose Capacities. Your instinct reveals which tool will stick.
