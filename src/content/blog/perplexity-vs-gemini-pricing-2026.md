# Perplexity vs Gemini Pricing 2026: $20 AI Search vs Google's $7.99+ Ladder

Perplexity and Gemini both answer questions with AI, but they're priced like different kinds of products. Perplexity keeps it simple — one $20/month Pro plan for cited, source-backed AI search. Gemini overhauled its pricing in 2026 into a six-tier ladder starting at $7.99/month, bundling a general-purpose AI assistant into Google's ecosystem. If your job is research with citations, the comparison isn't really about price — it's about what each $20 actually buys.

## The Plans Side by Side

| | Perplexity | Gemini |
|---|------------|--------|
| **Free** | $0, ~5 Pro searches/day | $0, base model, daily limits |
| **Entry paid** | — | AI Plus — $7.99/mo |
| **Core paid** | Pro — $20/mo ($16.67/mo annual) | AI Pro — $19.99/mo |
| **Power tier** | — | AI Ultra — ~$42/mo |
| **Team tier** | (not publicly listed) | Business — $20/user/mo |
| **What it is** | Cited AI search engine | General AI assistant + Google apps |

## The Free Tiers

**Perplexity Free ($0)** is genuinely useful, not a crippled trial. You get around 5 Pro-level searches per day plus unlimited standard answers with citations. For casual users asking a few questions a day, free covers most needs — the answers are accurate and the sources are linked.

**Gemini Free ($0)** gives access to Gemini's base model with daily usage limits — fine for casual questions, text generation, and image analysis, but you'll hit walls fast using it as a daily productivity tool.

Verdict on free: Perplexity if your need is *research with sources*; Gemini if you want a *general assistant* tied to Google.

## Core Paid: Pro ($20) vs AI Pro ($19.99)

**Perplexity Pro — $20/month** (or $200/year, about $16.67/month). Pro removes the ~5/day cap and gives unlimited Pro searches using advanced models (GPT-4, Claude, and Perplexity's own), plus deeper Focus modes across academic, financial, and specialized sources, and $5/month in API credits. Everything is built around one job: answering questions with reliable, cited sources. There are no team or enterprise tiers publicly listed.

**Gemini AI Pro — $19.99/month.** Almost the same headline price, very different product. AI Pro unlocks Gemini's most capable models plus deep integration with Google Workspace — Docs, Gmail, Sheets — and higher limits across the board. It's a general AI assistant that lives inside the tools many people already use daily, not a citation-first research engine.

The real difference at ~$20: Perplexity gives you a *better research engine*; Gemini gives you a *more capable general assistant inside Google*. They barely overlap.

## Where Gemini Goes Wider

Gemini's ladder has rungs Perplexity doesn't. **AI Plus at $7.99/month** is the cheapest paid AI assistant from any major provider — a strong pick if you want fewer limits without paying $20. **AI Ultra (~$42/month)** targets heavy users and developers, and **Business at $20/user/month** and **Enterprise at $30/user/month** bring Gemini into Workspace for teams. Perplexity, by contrast, bets on simplicity: one Pro plan, one job, done well.

## Which Should You Pick?

- **Need cited, source-backed research?** Perplexity Pro ($20/mo, $16.67 annual).
- **Want a cheap general AI assistant?** Gemini AI Plus ($7.99/mo).
- **Live inside Google Docs/Gmail/Sheets?** Gemini AI Pro ($19.99/mo).
- **Heavy user or developer?** Gemini AI Ultra (~$42/mo).
- **Team on Workspace?** Gemini Business ($20/user/mo).
- **Honest take?** They're complementary — Perplexity for research, Gemini for everyday assistant work in Google.

## The Bottom Line

Two ~$20 plans pointed at different problems. Perplexity Pro is a focused, citation-first research engine with one simple plan and an annual discount that drops it to $16.67/month. Gemini is a six-tier ladder — from a budget $7.99 assistant to a $42 power plan — built around a general AI woven into Google's apps. Pick Perplexity if your work is research that needs sources; pick Gemini if you want a flexible assistant where you already work. Many people pay for both, because they're not really competing for the same task.
