Trello has been a household name in project management since 2011. Known for its dead-simple Kanban boards, it's the tool many of us used *before* we knew what project management software was. But in 2026, with heavyweights like [Notion](/compare/notion-vs-trello), [ClickUp](/compare/clickup-vs-trello), and [Asana](/compare/trello-vs-asana) pushing the envelope, is Trello still relevant?

Let's find out.

## What Is Trello?

Trello is a visual project management tool built around Kanban boards. You create boards, add lists (columns), and move cards between them. It's owned by Atlassian — the same company behind Jira and Confluence.

## Key Features in 2026

### Kanban Boards
Trello's core. Drag-and-drop cards across lists to track progress. It's intuitive enough that your non-tech teammates will actually use it.

### Views Beyond Kanban
Trello now offers Timeline, Calendar, Table, Dashboard, and Map views — though most are locked behind the Premium plan.

### Power-Ups
Integrations with Slack, Google Drive, GitHub, and hundreds of other apps. Free plans get unlimited Power-Ups (a huge improvement from the old one-Power-Up limit).

### Butler Automation
Built-in automation that lets you create rules, scheduled commands, and buttons. No code needed. For example: "When a card is moved to Done, mark the due date as complete and notify the team."

### Trello AI (Beta)
Atlassian Intelligence is starting to roll out in Trello, offering AI-generated card descriptions, summaries, and suggested actions. Still early, but promising.

## Pros

- **Dead simple to learn** — 5-minute onboarding, no training needed
- **Great free plan** — Unlimited cards, unlimited Power-Ups, 10 boards
- **Reliable and fast** — Rarely goes down, snappy interface
- **Mobile app is excellent** — One of the best mobile PM experiences
- **Perfect for simple workflows** — Content calendars, personal tasks, light team coordination

## Cons

- **Limited for complex projects** — No native Gantt charts, dependencies are basic
- **Views locked behind Premium** — Timeline and Dashboard require $10/user/mo
- **Reporting is weak** — Dashboard view exists but lacks depth
- **No built-in docs** — You'll need a separate tool for documentation
- **Scaling issues** — Boards with 500+ cards become unwieldy

## Who Should Use Trello?

**Great for:**
- Small teams (2-10 people) with straightforward workflows
- Freelancers tracking personal projects
- Content teams managing editorial calendars
- Anyone who wants simplicity over power features

**Not great for:**
- Engineering teams needing sprint management (use [Linear](/tools/linear) instead)
- Large organizations with complex project hierarchies (try [Asana](/tools/asana) or [Monday.com](/tools/monday))
- Teams wanting an all-in-one workspace (consider [Notion](/tools/notion) or [ClickUp](/tools/clickup))

## The Verdict: 7.5/10

Trello remains the best project management tool for people who don't want to think about project management. It does one thing — Kanban boards — and does it beautifully.

But if your needs have outgrown "move cards left to right," you'll hit Trello's ceiling fast. The lack of native docs, limited reporting, and view restrictions on the free plan make it hard to recommend as your *only* tool in 2026.

**Bottom line:** Use Trello if simplicity is your priority. Switch to [ClickUp](/tools/clickup) or [Notion](/tools/notion) if you need more firepower.
