**[Typeform](/tools/typeform/)** turns forms into conversations. **Google Forms** turns forms into... forms. Both collect data. But they approach the job from completely different directions, and the gap between them is wider than you might expect.

Typeform charges $29-99/month for a polished, one-question-at-a-time experience designed to maximize completion rates. Google Forms costs nothing, has no response limits, and works well enough for most internal use cases. The real question is whether the premium experience justifies the price — or whether "good enough" is actually good enough.

| Analytics | Built-in response analytics, drop-off tracking | Basic summary charts |

## Pricing: Free vs $29-99/Month

Google Forms has no pricing page because there's nothing to price. It's free for anyone with a Google account. Unlimited forms, unlimited responses, unlimited collaborators. No premium tier to upsell you into. What you see is what you get.

Typeform's pricing is structured around response volume:

- **Basic:** $29/month — 100 responses, 1 user, basic integrations
- **Plus:** $59/month — 1,000 responses, 3 users, file uploads, custom branding removal
- **Business:** $99/month — 10,000 responses, 5 users, priority support, advanced analytics

All prices reflect annual billing. Monthly billing costs roughly 20% more.

The response caps matter. If you're running a popular survey or a lead generation form on a high-traffic page, 100 responses per month disappears fast. At that point, you're looking at $59/month minimum — and you need to decide whether the experience difference is worth $700+ per year over a free alternative.

## Design and User Experience

This is where the two tools diverge most sharply.

**Typeform** presents one question per screen with smooth transitions between them. The experience feels more like a chat than a form. Users stay focused, distractions disappear, and completion rates go up — Typeform regularly claims 2x higher completion than traditional forms, and third-party testing generally supports that for consumer-facing surveys under 15 questions.

You get full control over colors, fonts, background images, and button styles. Every form can look like an extension of your brand. For client-facing work — lead capture, customer feedback, event registrations — this polish matters.

**Google Forms** puts all questions on a single scrollable page (or across sections you define manually). The interface is clean and functional, but it looks like Google Forms. There's no way around that. You can add a header image and change the accent color, but the Google branding and layout structure remain.

For internal surveys, team feedback, or quick data collection where nobody cares what the form looks like, this is perfectly fine. For a customer-facing brand touchpoint, it feels like you didn't try.

**Winner:** Typeform for anything external or brand-sensitive. Google Forms for anything internal or utilitarian.

## Response Analytics

Typeform gives you built-in analytics that go beyond basic tallies. You can see completion rates, average time to complete, drop-off points (which question makes people quit), and response trends over time. This data is genuinely useful for optimizing lead gen forms or customer surveys.

Google Forms gives you a summary page with auto-generated charts — pie charts for multiple choice, bar charts for checkboxes, a list of text responses. It's enough to read results, but there's no funnel analysis, no drop-off tracking, and no time-based trends. For deeper analysis, you export to Google Sheets and build it yourself.

If you're collecting data that feeds into business decisions and you want to understand how people interact with the form itself, Typeform's analytics justify part of the cost. If you just need to see the answers, Google Forms handles it.

## Integrations

Typeform connects natively with over 100 tools including [Zapier](/tools/zapier/), [HubSpot](/tools/hubspot/), [Slack](/tools/slack/), [Mailchimp](/tools/mailchimp/), [Notion](/tools/notion/), Salesforce, and Google Sheets. The integrations are well-built — the HubSpot CRM integration, for example, maps form fields directly to contact properties without requiring a third-party connector.

Typeform also supports payment collection through Stripe and PayPal on paid plans, letting you accept payments directly within the form flow. This makes it viable for order forms, donation pages, and paid event registrations.

Google Forms integrates with Google Sheets automatically (every response appears as a row in real time) and connects to Google Drive for file uploads. Beyond the Google ecosystem, your options are limited to third-party tools like Zapier or Apps Script automations. There's no native payment collection at all.

If your workflow already lives in Google Workspace, the Sheets integration might be all you need. If you're feeding form data into a CRM, marketing platform, or payment system, Typeform's native connections save real setup time.

## Collaboration and Sharing

Google Forms wins here cleanly. Share a form with anyone via link, email, or embed. Add collaborators with the same ease as sharing a Google Doc. No user seat limits, no plan-tier restrictions. If your team lives in Google Workspace, collaboration is seamless.

Typeform limits collaborators based on your plan — 1 user on Basic, 3 on Plus, 5 on Business. Adding more requires an Enterprise plan. For solo creators or small teams, that's fine. For organizations where multiple departments need to build and manage forms, the seat limits add friction.

## Mobile Experience

Both tools produce forms that work on mobile devices, but the experience differs.

Typeform's one-question-at-a-time format was essentially designed for mobile. Each question fills the screen naturally, buttons are tap-friendly, and transitions feel smooth. Mobile completion rates tend to be strong.

Google Forms renders well on mobile but doesn't adapt its format. You're scrolling through the same long-form layout you'd see on desktop, just narrower. It works, but it doesn't feel intentional.

## Who Should Choose Typeform

Pick Typeform if:

- **You're building customer-facing forms.** Lead capture, feedback surveys, event registrations, or anything where the form represents your brand.
- **Completion rates matter to your bottom line.** The conversational format genuinely converts better for short-to-medium surveys.
- **You need payment collection.** Stripe and PayPal integration within the form flow is something Google Forms simply doesn't offer.
- **You want actionable analytics.** Drop-off tracking and completion funnels help you optimize forms over time.

## Who Should Choose Google Forms

Pick Google Forms if:

- **Budget is zero.** No contest. Free, unlimited, no catches.
- **You need internal or team surveys.** Employee feedback, meeting polls, simple data collection — Google Forms handles these perfectly.
- **Your team lives in Google Workspace.** Sheets integration, easy sharing, no new tool to learn.
- **Volume is high and budget is low.** If you're collecting thousands of responses and can't justify $59-99/month, Google Forms doesn't blink.

## The Bottom Line

Typeform is a better experience. Google Forms is a better deal. If your forms are customer-facing, brand matters, and you need integrations beyond the Google ecosystem, Typeform earns its price. If you need functional data collection without spending a dollar, Google Forms does the job without complaints.

The decision usually comes down to one question: will the person filling out this form judge your brand by it? If yes, Typeform. If no, Google Forms.
