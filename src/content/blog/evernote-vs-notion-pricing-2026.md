# Evernote vs Notion Pricing 2026: $10.83 Note App vs $10 Workspace

Evernote and Notion both start their paid tiers near $10/month, but they're aimed at different jobs. Evernote is a focused note-taking app — capture, search, and retrieve notes, with a free tier that has been dramatically scaled back. Notion is an all-in-one workspace — notes, databases, wikis, and project management in one flexible canvas. The matching ~$10 price hides a real question: do you want a notebook, or a workspace?

## The Plans Side by Side

| | Evernote | Notion |
|---|----------|--------|
| **Free** | 50 notes total, 1 device | $0, generous personal use |
| **Entry paid** | Starter — $10.83/mo (annual) | Plus — $10/user/mo (annual) |
| **Higher tier** | Advanced — $14.99/mo | Business — $18/user/mo (annual) |
| **What it is** | Focused note-taking app | All-in-one workspace |

## The Free Tiers (This Is the Big Story)

**Evernote Free** has been gutted. It now caps you at **50 notes total** — not per month, but for the entire lifetime of the account — plus **one device** and **one notebook**. No sync between phone and laptop, no folder structure. This is a steep downgrade from the old unlimited-notes, two-device free tier, and it's pushed many long-time users to either pay or leave.

**Notion Free ($0)** is the opposite story — surprisingly capable. Unlimited personal pages and blocks, plenty of room for note-taking, journaling, and light project management. The main limits are guest sharing (10 people), file upload size, and page history.

Verdict on free: this is lopsided. Notion's free plan is a real long-term home; Evernote's free plan is now effectively a trial.

## Entry Paid: Starter ($10.83) vs Plus ($10)

**Evernote Starter — $10.83/month** (billed $129.99/year; roughly $14.99 if paid monthly). Starter removes the most painful free-tier restrictions — multi-device sync, more notebooks, larger limits. It's Evernote's sweet spot for casual users who want reliable note-taking without paying premium prices: capture ideas, write notes, save articles. If your workflow is straightforward note-keeping, Starter covers it.

**Notion Plus — $10/user/month** (annual; $12 monthly). Plus unlocks unlimited file uploads, more guests, and full Notion AI access on top of an already-powerful free tier. But you're paying for far more than notes — databases, linked views, wikis, and project boards. That power is also the trade-off: Notion has a learning curve Evernote doesn't.

The real difference: Evernote does *one thing* — notes — with almost no setup. Notion does *many things* but asks you to build your own system. At nearly the same price, you're choosing simplicity vs flexibility.

## Where They Go Higher

**Evernote Advanced ($14.99/month, $179.99/year)** adds AI features — summarizing long notes, finding related content, generating drafts — for power note-takers. **Notion Business ($18/user/month annual, $21 monthly)** scales toward teams and companies with advanced permissions, analytics, and collaboration. So Evernote's upgrade buys *smarter notes*; Notion's buys *bigger team capability*.

## Which Should You Pick?

- **Want a simple, reliable note app with zero setup?** Evernote Starter ($10.83/mo).
- **Want one flexible workspace for notes, docs, and projects?** Notion Plus ($10/user/mo).
- **Note-taker who wants AI summaries and search?** Evernote Advanced ($14.99/mo).
- **Growing team needing permissions and collaboration?** Notion Business ($18/user/mo).
- **On a budget and fine with limits?** Notion Free is far more generous than Evernote Free.
- **Honest take?** If you only need notes, Evernote is simpler; if you want one tool for everything, Notion wins on value.

## The Bottom Line

Nearly the same entry price, opposite philosophies. Evernote is a focused note app — fast, simple, no setup — but its free tier is now so limited that you'll likely need the $10.83 Starter plan to use it seriously. Notion is an all-in-one workspace with a genuinely usable free tier and a $10 Plus plan that does far more than notes, at the cost of a learning curve. Pick Evernote if you want a clean notebook and nothing more; pick Notion if you want one canvas for notes, databases, and projects.
