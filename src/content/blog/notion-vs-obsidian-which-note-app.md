**Notion** and **Obsidian** are both popular tools in their category, but they serve different needs and audiences. This guide compares their features, pricing, and best use cases to help you choose the right one.

Choosing between Notion and Obsidian is one of the most debated decisions in the productivity space. Both are powerful, but they take fundamentally different approaches to [note-taking](/category/note-taking/) and knowledge management. Let's break down which one fits your needs.

## The Core Difference

**Notion** is a cloud-first, all-in-one workspace. It combines notes, databases, [project management](/category/project-management/), and wikis into a single platform. Everything lives on Notion's servers, and collaboration is built into every feature.

**Obsidian** is a local-first Markdown editor. Your notes are plain `.md` files stored on your device. It's built for personal knowledge management with features like backlinks, graph view, and a massive plugin ecosystem.

## Features Comparison

### Note-Taking & Editing
Notion uses a block-based editor with rich media support — databases, embeds, toggles, and callouts. Obsidian uses pure Markdown with optional plugins for extended formatting.

If you want drag-and-drop flexibility with visual blocks, Notion wins. If you want speed and portability with Markdown files you own, Obsidian is the clear choice.

### Knowledge Management
Obsidian's **graph view** and **bidirectional linking** [make](/tools/make/) it a powerhouse for building a personal knowledge base (often called a "second brain"). You can visualize how your notes connect and discover relationships you didn't know existed.

Notion offers linked databases and relations between pages, but it's more structured. You're working with databases and properties rather than freeform connections.

### Collaboration
Notion is built for teams. Real-time editing, comments, permissions, and shared workspaces [make](/tools/make/) it ideal for collaborative work.

Obsidian is primarily a solo tool. While Obsidian Publish lets you share notes as a website, real-time collaboration isn't a core feature.

### Offline Access
Obsidian works 100% offline since your files are local. Notion requires an internet connection for most features, though it has improved offline support recently.

### Customization
Obsidian's plugin ecosystem is extraordinary — over 1,500 community plugins for everything from Kanban boards to spaced repetition. You can make Obsidian do almost anything.

Notion is customizable through its block system and database templates, but you can't extend its core functionality the way you can with Obsidian.

## Pricing

| Plan | Notion | Obsidian |
|------|--------|----------|
| Free | ✅ Generous free tier | ✅ Free for personal use |
| Paid | $10/user/mo (Starter) | $4/mo (Sync) |
| Team | $18/user/mo (Business) | $8/mo (Publish) |

Obsidian is significantly cheaper, especially since the core app is free forever. You only pay for optional Sync and Publish services.

## Who Should Choose What?

**Choose Notion if you:**
- Work with a team and need collaboration
- Want an all-in-one workspace (notes + tasks + databases)
- Prefer a visual, block-based editor
- Don't mind cloud storage

**Choose Obsidian if you:**
- Value data ownership and local-first storage
- Build personal knowledge bases with heavy linking
- Want extreme customization through plugins
- Prefer writing in Markdown

## The Verdict

There's no wrong choice here — it depends on your priorities. Notion excels as a **collaborative workspace**, while Obsidian shines as a **personal knowledge management tool**.

Many power users actually use both: Obsidian for personal notes and thinking, Notion for team projects and shared documentation.
