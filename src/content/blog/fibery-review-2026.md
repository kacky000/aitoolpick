**Fibery** is a work management platform that tries to be the tool you'd build yourself if you had unlimited engineering time. It combines project management, documents, databases, reports, and AI into a single workspace where everything connects to everything else. It's not as well-known as Notion or ClickUp, but the teams that use it tend to become vocal advocates.

## What Is Fibery?

Fibery is a connected workspace for teams that need to manage work across multiple dimensions — projects, features, bugs, customers, feedback, goals — and see how they all relate. Unlike tools that force you into a fixed structure (like Jira's issue hierarchy or Asana's project/task model), Fibery lets you define your own entity types and relationships.

You define the objects your team cares about (Features, Bugs, Customers, Sprints, OKRs), set up relationships between them, and view everything through boards, timelines, lists, charts, and docs.

## Key Features

### Customizable Workspaces

Fibery's workspace is built from **Spaces**, each containing **Databases** (entity types) with **Views**. You can create a Product space with Features, Bugs, and Releases databases, then create an Engineering space with Sprints and Tasks — and link them together.

The schema is yours to define. Need to track "Customer Segments" linked to "Revenue Targets" linked to "Feature Priorities"? Build it in minutes. This flexibility is what sets Fibery apart from more rigid tools.

### Bi-Directional Relations

Every relationship in Fibery is bi-directional. Link a Bug to a Feature, and that Feature automatically shows the Bug in its related items. Link a Customer to a piece of Feedback, and the Feedback shows on the Customer's profile.

This sounds simple, but it's surprisingly rare in work management tools. Most competitors offer one-way links or require manual cross-referencing. Fibery's automatic bi-directional linking means your data stays consistent without extra effort.

For comparison, [Notion](/blog/notion-review-2026) supports relations and rollups in databases, but the setup is more manual and the linking less fluid than Fibery's approach.

### Fibery AI

Fibery integrates AI directly into its workflow — not as a chatbot sidebar, but as a tool that operates on your actual work data.

What Fibery AI can do:
- **Summarize feedback clusters** — group 200 customer feedback entries by theme automatically
- **Generate reports** — ask "What features had the most bugs last sprint?" and get a structured answer
- **Draft documents** — create specs, PRDs, or status updates from database data
- **Automate field values** — auto-classify incoming items based on content
- **Formula assistance** — write complex filters and formulas in natural language

The AI works best when your workspace has rich, connected data. The more relationships and context you build, the more useful AI queries become.

### Reports and Charts

Fibery includes a built-in reporting engine that can aggregate data across any database. Create burndown charts, velocity reports, customer feedback heatmaps, or OKR progress dashboards — all pulling from live data.

Reports are first-class entities, not afterthoughts. You can create calculated fields, group by any property, filter by relations, and display results as charts, tables, or numbers. For teams that currently export data to spreadsheets for analysis, this alone might justify the switch.

### Automations

Fibery supports rule-based automations: when a condition is met, trigger an action. Examples include moving items between states, sending notifications, updating related entities, or running Fibery AI prompts.

Automations work across related databases — when a Bug's status changes to "Fixed," automatically update the linked Feature's bug count. These cross-entity automations are powerful for teams with complex workflows.

### Documents

Every entity in Fibery has a built-in rich-text document. Feature specs, meeting notes, design briefs — they all live directly on the relevant entity, with embeds, mentions, and bi-directional links.

| Solo | $10/user/month | Single user, full features, AI credits |
| Team | $17/user/month | Collaboration, permissions, integrations |
| Enterprise | Custom | SSO, audit log, priority support |

The Solo plan at $10/user/month is generous — you get the full workspace builder, all views, automations, and AI features. The Team plan at $17/user/month adds multi-user collaboration, permissions, and third-party integrations.

Compared to [ClickUp vs Monday](/compare/clickup-vs-monday) pricing, Fibery sits in a similar range but offers significantly more customization. You're paying for flexibility that those tools simply don't provide.

No per-workspace or per-database limits on paid plans. You build as much as you need.

## Pros

- **Unmatched flexibility** — define your own entity types, relationships, and views without code
- **True bi-directional relations** — everything connects automatically
- **AI that operates on work data** — not just a writing assistant, but a data analysis tool
- **Powerful reporting** — built-in charts and aggregations across any database
- **Clean, focused UI** — less cluttered than ClickUp, more structured than Notion
- **Active development** — meaningful updates ship regularly
- **Fair pricing** — no per-feature paywalls or artificial limits

## Cons

- **Steep learning curve** — the flexibility means more upfront setup time
- **Small community** — fewer templates, tutorials, and integrations than major competitors
- **Limited integrations** — works with Slack, GitHub, GitLab, Intercom, and Jira, but the list is shorter than ClickUp or Monday
- **No mobile app** — web-only as of mid-2026 (responsive web works on mobile, but no native app)
- **Onboarding takes effort** — you need to design your workspace structure before it becomes useful
- **Single-player mode is overkill** — Fibery's strength is in connected team data; solo users may find it overengineered

## Who Is Fibery Best For?

Fibery shines for **product teams, engineering teams, and agencies** that need to manage interconnected workflows. If your work involves multiple entity types with complex relationships — features tied to customer feedback tied to roadmap items tied to sprint tasks — Fibery handles this natively.

It's particularly strong for:
- **Product managers** who need feedback-to-feature traceability
- **Engineering leads** managing sprints, bugs, and technical debt across multiple projects
- **Agencies** tracking clients, projects, deliverables, and team capacity in one system
- **Operations teams** building custom workflows that off-the-shelf tools can't accommodate

Fibery is *not* the right choice for individuals looking for a simple task manager, teams that want zero setup (use Asana or Monday instead), or organizations that need extensive third-party integrations.

## Verdict

Fibery occupies a unique position: more structured than Notion, more flexible than Monday or Asana, more approachable than building on Airtable. It's the work management tool for teams that have outgrown rigid project managers but don't want to maintain a custom internal tool.

The learning curve is real, and the smaller ecosystem means less hand-holding during setup. But if you invest the time to model your workspace properly, Fibery becomes the kind of tool that makes you wonder how you worked without it. For teams managing complex, interconnected work — especially product and engineering teams — it's worth a serious look.
