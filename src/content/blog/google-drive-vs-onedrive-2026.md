**Google Drive** and **OneDrive** are the two dominant cloud storage platforms, but they serve different workflows and ecosystems. This guide compares their pricing, features, and real-world strengths so you can pick the one that actually fits how you work.

Both services have matured significantly. Google Drive leans into web-first collaboration with Google Workspace, while OneDrive doubles down on Microsoft 365 integration and desktop sync. The right choice depends less on raw specs and more on which ecosystem you already live in.

## Free Storage: Google Drive Starts Ahead

Google gives you 15 GB free, though that total is shared across Gmail, Drive, and Google Photos. OneDrive offers 5 GB free with a Microsoft account. For most people, 15 GB goes further — especially if you keep your inbox and photos lean.

That said, the 15 GB cap includes every attachment sitting in your Gmail, which can quietly eat into your storage. OneDrive's 5 GB is purely file storage, so it is more predictable even if smaller.

## Pricing: OneDrive Wins on Value

At the entry level, both charge $1.99/month for 100 GB. But the story changes at higher tiers.

Google One's 2 TB plan costs $9.99/month and includes VPN access, expanded Google Photos storage, and the ability to share with up to five family members (at $13.99/month for the family plan).

Microsoft 365 Personal at $6.99/month gives you 1 TB of OneDrive storage plus full desktop versions of Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Outlook. The Family plan at $9.99/month covers six users with 1 TB each — that is 6 TB total for the price of Google's 2 TB individual plan.

If you need Office apps anyway, Microsoft 365 is hard to beat on sheer value per dollar.

## Collaboration: Google Drive Is Still King

Google's real-time collaboration remains the gold standard. Multiple users editing a Google Doc simultaneously is seamless — cursor tracking, inline comments, suggestion mode, and version history all work without friction. Sheets and Slides offer the same experience.

OneDrive supports co-authoring in the web versions of Word, Excel, and PowerPoint, and it has improved substantially. But the experience is not quite as smooth as Google's, particularly with larger documents or complex spreadsheets. Desktop Office apps support real-time co-authoring too, though occasional sync conflicts still surface.

If your team collaborates heavily on documents throughout the day, Google Drive and its Workspace suite remain the stronger choice.

## Desktop and Offline Access: OneDrive Has the Edge

OneDrive's Files On-Demand feature on Windows is excellent. Files appear in File Explorer as if they are local, but only download when you open them. You can pin files or folders for offline access, and the sync engine is deeply integrated into the OS. On macOS, it works similarly through the OneDrive app.

Google Drive for Desktop offers comparable functionality, but it has historically been less reliable on Windows. The sync client works well enough, but OneDrive's integration with Windows File Explorer feels native because it literally is — Microsoft built it into the operating system.

For users who spend most of their time in desktop apps and need reliable offline access, OneDrive has a clear advantage.

## File Sharing and Permissions

Both platforms handle file sharing well. Google Drive lets you share files or folders via link with view, comment, or edit permissions. You can set expiration dates on shared links and restrict downloading or copying. Sharing is straightforward and most people already understand how it works.

OneDrive offers similar link-based sharing with comparable permission controls. Microsoft 365 Business plans add more granular sharing policies and compliance features, which matters for enterprise use.

For personal and small team use, both are equally capable. For enterprise governance, OneDrive with Microsoft 365 Business has more administrative controls out of the box.

## Security: OneDrive's Personal Vault

OneDrive includes a feature called Personal Vault — a protected folder that requires additional identity verification (fingerprint, PIN, or two-factor authentication) to access. It locks automatically after a period of inactivity. This is useful for storing sensitive documents like tax returns, ID scans, or financial records.

Google Drive does not have an equivalent feature. You get standard Google account security — two-factor authentication, suspicious activity alerts, and encryption in transit and at rest — but no special locked folder within Drive itself.

Both platforms encrypt files in transit and at rest. For most users, either is secure enough. But if you want that extra layer for sensitive files without using a separate tool, OneDrive's Personal Vault is a genuine differentiator.

## Photo Storage

Google Drive connects to Google Photos, which offers powerful search (by location, face, object), automatic organization, and editing tools. Photos stored in Google Photos count toward your 15 GB free limit (or your Google One quota).

OneDrive integrates with the Photos app on Windows and offers its own photo view with some organization features, but it is not as sophisticated as Google Photos for search and auto-tagging.

If photo management matters to you, Google's ecosystem is significantly ahead.

## Mobile Apps

Both apps work well on iOS and Android. Google Drive's mobile app integrates tightly with Docs, Sheets, and Slides — you can create and edit documents directly. OneDrive's app connects to the mobile versions of Word, Excel, and PowerPoint, and includes a document scanner.

The mobile experience is strong on both sides. Google's app feels slightly more polished on Android (unsurprisingly), while OneDrive performs well on both platforms.

## Who Should Use Google Drive

- Teams that collaborate heavily on documents in real time
- Users already embedded in the Google ecosystem (Gmail, Calendar, Meet)
- Anyone who wants 15 GB free without paying
- Photographers and users who rely on Google Photos
- Web-first workflows where browser-based editing is the default
- If you are evaluating cloud storage more broadly, see our [Google Drive review](/blog/google-drive-review-2026/) for a deeper look

## Who Should Use OneDrive

- Anyone already paying for Microsoft 365 (you have 1 TB included)
- Desktop-heavy users who need reliable offline sync on Windows
- Families — the Microsoft 365 Family plan at $9.99/month for 6 TB is unmatched value
- Users who want Personal Vault for sensitive documents
- Organizations using SharePoint, Teams, or other Microsoft 365 tools

## Should You Consider Other Options?

If neither Google nor Microsoft's ecosystem appeals to you, [Dropbox](/blog/dropbox-review-2026/) is a strong independent alternative with excellent sync and file management. See our [Dropbox vs Google Drive comparison](/blog/dropbox-vs-google-drive-2026/) for a detailed breakdown.

For privacy-focused users, services like Proton Drive or Tresorit offer end-to-end encryption that neither Google Drive nor OneDrive provides by default.

## The Bottom Line

Google Drive is the better choice for collaboration-first teams and users who live in the browser. Its real-time editing, generous free tier, and tight integration with Google Workspace make it the default for web-centric workflows.

OneDrive is the better choice for Microsoft 365 users, desktop-heavy workflows, and families who want the most storage per dollar. If you already pay for Office apps, OneDrive's 1 TB of included storage makes it effectively free.

Most users will find their choice is already made by whichever ecosystem they use day to day. If you are genuinely starting from scratch, Google Drive's free tier and collaboration tools give it a slight edge for individuals, while OneDrive's Microsoft 365 bundle wins for families and anyone who needs Office apps.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Google Drive or OneDrive better?

It depends on your workflow. Google Drive excels at real-time collaboration and web-based editing, while OneDrive is stronger for desktop sync and Microsoft 365 integration. Compare features and pricing above to find the best fit.

### Can I use Google Drive and OneDrive together?

Yes, many people use both — Google Drive for collaborative work and OneDrive for personal file storage or Office documents. Both sync clients can run side by side on Windows and macOS.

### Which is cheaper, Google Drive or OneDrive?

At 100 GB, both cost $1.99/month. For larger storage, OneDrive offers better value through Microsoft 365 Personal (1 TB for $6.99/month), especially since it includes the full Office suite. Google's 2 TB plan at $9.99/month does not include equivalent desktop apps.
