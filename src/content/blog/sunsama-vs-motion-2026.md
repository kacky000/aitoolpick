# Sunsama vs Motion 2026: Intentional Planning or AI Autopilot?

Sunsama and Motion both want to fix the same problem: your calendar is a mess, your task list keeps growing, and you never feel like you got the right things done. But they take completely opposite approaches to solving it.

[Sunsama](/tools/sunsama/) gives you a structured daily planning ritual where you decide what matters. [Motion](/tools/motion/) hands that decision to an AI engine that schedules everything for you automatically.

Here's how they actually compare when you use them day to day.

## How They Work (The Core Difference)

### Sunsama: The Daily Planning Ritual

Every morning, Sunsama walks you through a guided planning session. You pull tasks from your existing tools — Todoist, Asana, Linear, Jira, ClickUp, Notion, Gmail, Slack — and decide what you'll work on today. Then you timebox each task by dragging it onto your calendar.

At the end of the day, there's a shutdown ritual. You review what you finished, push unfinished tasks to tomorrow, and close the day intentionally.

This works well if you:
- Want to be deliberate about what makes it onto your plate
- Already use a task manager and need a planning layer on top
- Tend to overcommit and need guardrails (Sunsama warns when you've planned too much)
- Value the psychological benefit of a defined start and end to your workday

The downside: you're the one making every scheduling decision. Sunsama won't tell you when to work on things — it just gives you a structured space to decide.

### Motion: AI Does the Scheduling

Motion takes a fundamentally different approach. You dump in your tasks with deadlines and estimated durations, and its AI engine figures out when you should work on each one. It rearranges your calendar in real time as meetings get added or priorities shift.

Think of it as an executive assistant that constantly optimizes your schedule. If a meeting gets canceled, Motion automatically fills that gap with your highest-priority task. If something is due tomorrow and you haven't started, it bumps less urgent work.

This works well if you:
- Have a lot of tasks with hard deadlines
- Hate the act of scheduling and want someone (something) to just do it
- Manage projects where task dependencies matter
- Want automatic reprioritization when your day gets disrupted

The downside: you have to trust the AI. Sometimes Motion's scheduling decisions feel wrong, and overriding them defeats the purpose. You also need to be disciplined about entering accurate time estimates and deadlines — garbage in, garbage out.

## Feature Comparison

### Integrations

**Sunsama** is built to be the layer on top of your existing tools. It pulls tasks from:
- Todoist, Asana, Linear, Jira, ClickUp
- Notion databases
- Gmail and Outlook (turn emails into tasks)
- Slack
- Google Calendar and Outlook Calendar

**Motion** integrates with calendars (Google, Outlook) and has its own built-in task and project management. It also connects to Zapier for broader automation, but it doesn't pull tasks natively from as many tools as Sunsama does.

**Winner: Sunsama** if you already have a task management stack. **Motion** if you want one tool to replace multiple tools.

### Calendar Management

Sunsama displays your calendar alongside your task list, and you drag tasks onto time slots. It's visual and manual — you see your day and decide where things fit.

Motion *is* the calendar. It doesn't just display your schedule; it builds it. Tasks automatically slot into open calendar blocks based on priority, deadline, and estimated duration. Your calendar is always a live, optimized view of what you should be doing right now.

**Winner: Motion** for hands-off scheduling. **Sunsama** for hands-on control.

### Team Collaboration

Sunsama is primarily a personal planning tool. You can see teammates' availability and there's basic team functionality, but it's not designed for project management.

Motion includes full project management with task dependencies, automated scheduling across team members, and workload balancing. If a team member is overloaded, Motion redistributes tasks automatically.

**Winner: Motion** — and it's not close for team use cases.

### Focus and Wellbeing

Sunsama has a feature Motion doesn't: it actively protects you from overwork. When your planned day exceeds your set working hours, Sunsama shows a warning. The daily shutdown ritual reinforces work-life boundaries.

Motion optimizes for getting everything done before deadlines. It'll schedule work right up to your limits if that's what the math requires.

**Winner: Sunsama** for work-life balance. Motion can feel relentless.

## Who Should Pick Sunsama

- You already use Todoist, Asana, Jira, or similar tools and want a planning layer
- You prefer deciding your own priorities rather than letting an algorithm choose
- You value the ritual of intentional daily planning and a defined shutdown routine
- You tend to overcommit and need a tool that pushes back

## Who Should Pick Motion

- You have many tasks with hard deadlines and don't want to manually schedule them
- You manage projects with task dependencies across a team
- You want one tool to replace your calendar + task manager + project manager
- You need something more like [Clockwise](/tools/clockwise/) but with full task management built in

## The Verdict

These tools solve the same problem with opposite philosophies, so the choice comes down to how you like to work.

**Pick Sunsama** if you want to be in the driver's seat. The guided planning ritual makes you more intentional, and the integrations mean you don't have to leave your existing tools behind. At $20/month, you're paying for focus and clarity — not automation.

**Pick Motion** if you want the machine to handle scheduling. It's genuinely impressive at juggling deadlines and reprioritizing on the fly, especially for teams. At $19/month for individuals (or $12/user for teams), the AI scheduling pays for itself if it saves you even 15 minutes of planning per day.

Both offer trials — 14 days for [Sunsama](/tools/sunsama/), 7 days for [Motion](/tools/motion/). Start with whichever philosophy resonates, and you'll know within a week whether it sticks.
