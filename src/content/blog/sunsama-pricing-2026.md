[Sunsama](/tools/sunsama/) charges $20/month on the annual plan ($240/year) or $25/month if you pay monthly. There's one plan. No tiers, no feature gates, no upsells. You either pay for the full product or you don't use it.

That simplicity is refreshing — but the price tag puts Sunsama in premium territory for a daily planner. Todoist's Pro plan costs $5/month. TickTick Premium is $36/year. [Reclaim.ai](/tools/reclaim/) has a free tier. So what exactly are you paying for?

## Sunsama Pricing at a Glance

| | Monthly | Annual |
|--|---------|--------|
| Per user | $25/mo | $20/mo ($240/year) |
| 2 users | $50/mo | $40/mo ($480/year) |
| 5 users | $125/mo | $100/mo ($1,200/year) |
| Free trial | 14 days | 14 days |
| Free plan | ❌ | ❌ |

There's no free plan. The 14-day trial gives you full access with no credit card required, which is enough time to test the daily planning workflow. But if you don't actively use it during those two weeks, you'll miss the window.

## The 2026 Price Increase

Sunsama raised its annual price from $16/month to $20/month in early 2026 — a 25% increase. The monthly price went from $20 to $25. Existing subscribers on annual plans were grandfathered at the old rate through their billing cycle, but new signups pay the new price.

The increase reflects Sunsama's position: they're not competing on price, and they're not trying to be the cheapest option. They're betting that users who find value in the daily planning ritual will pay more for it.

For context, [Motion](/tools/motion/) charges $19/month (annual) for AI-powered scheduling, and [Reclaim.ai](/tools/reclaim/) offers a free tier with AI time blocking. The gap between Sunsama and its closest competitors has widened.

## What's Included (Everything)

Since there's only one plan, every feature is unlocked from day one:

**Daily Planning**
- Guided morning planning ritual
- Timeboxing — drag tasks onto your calendar as time blocks
- Time estimates with overcommitment warnings
- Daily shutdown routine
- Focus mode (single-task view with timer)

**Integrations**
- Pull tasks from Todoist, Asana, Linear, Jira, Trello, ClickUp, Notion
- Pull emails from Gmail as tasks
- Sync with Google Calendar and Outlook
- Turn Slack messages into tasks
- Two-way sync (complete in Sunsama, marks complete in source tool)

**Weekly Review**
- Structured review of planned vs. actual hours
- Task carry-over analysis
- Recurring patterns identification
- Next-week intention setting

**Other**
- Cross-device sync (desktop app, web, mobile)
- Keyboard shortcuts throughout
- Custom themes
- Unlimited task history

There are no add-ons, no premium integrations, no advanced features locked behind a higher tier. The single-plan model means you never wonder if you're missing something.

## Hidden Costs

There aren't any. No per-integration fees, no storage limits, no API charges. The price you see is the price you pay.

The one indirect cost worth noting: Sunsama works best when paired with other tools. It's not a replacement for your task manager — it's a planning layer on top of it. So you'll likely keep paying for Todoist, Asana, Linear, or whatever you use for task management, plus Sunsama on top. Your total productivity stack cost goes up.

## Is $20/Month Worth It?

This depends entirely on how you work and what problem you're solving.

### Worth it if:

**You juggle tasks across multiple tools.** If your work arrives via Asana tickets, Slack messages, Gmail threads, and Jira issues, Sunsama consolidates everything into a single daily view. Without it, you're tab-switching between four tools to figure out what to do next. The integration hub alone can save 20-30 minutes of daily context switching.

**You chronically overplan your days.** Sunsama's overcommitment warning — which flags when your planned tasks exceed your target work hours — is a genuine behavioral intervention. Over time, you develop more accurate intuitions about what a realistic day looks like. That's a skill that compounds.

**You want a shutdown ritual.** The end-of-day review that Sunsama prompts isn't something you'll do on your own. It asks you to move incomplete tasks, reflect on what got done, and consciously end the workday. For people who struggle to stop working, this structure is therapeutic.

For a full breakdown of these features in action, see our [Sunsama Review 2026](/blog/sunsama-review-2026).

### Not worth it if:

**You mainly need a task list.** If your workflow is "write down tasks, check them off," Todoist at $5/month or TickTick at $3/month does this better and cheaper. Sunsama's value is in the planning ritual, not the task management.

**You need project management.** Sunsama doesn't do project views, Gantt charts, team assignments, or workload management. If you're a project manager coordinating a team, look at ClickUp, Asana, or Monday.com instead.

**Budget is tight.** At $240/year, Sunsama is the most expensive daily planner on the market. If that amount causes friction, the cheaper alternatives below cover the essentials.

## Team Pricing

Sunsama charges the same per-user rate for teams — there's no team discount. Two users cost $40/month (annual), five users cost $100/month. There's no admin console, no team analytics dashboard, and no shared workspace features designed for team coordination.

This is intentional. Sunsama is a personal planning tool, not a team collaboration platform. Each person gets their own daily plan, their own weekly review, and their own integrations. The "team" use case is really just multiple individuals on the same billing account.

If you need team-level scheduling coordination, Reclaim.ai's team features or Motion's team plan are better suited.

## The Verdict

Sunsama at $20/month is expensive for what it is — a daily planner — but fairly priced for what it does. The guided planning ritual, the integration hub that consolidates scattered tasks, and the overcommitment prevention are genuinely useful behaviors that most people won't build on their own.

Start with the 14-day free trial. Use it every morning for two weeks. If the daily planning ritual clicks and you find yourself more intentional about your time, the $20/month pays for itself in recovered focus. If you forget to open it after day three, save the money and stick with Todoist.
