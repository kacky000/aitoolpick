**Wrike** offers five pricing tiers in 2026, from a free plan for small groups to custom enterprise options. This guide covers what each plan includes, what changed in 2026, and how to decide which tier actually makes sense for your team.

Wrike made a notable change at the start of 2026 by retiring its Enterprise plan and replacing it with Apex — a new top-tier option aimed at large organizations with complex portfolios. If you've been looking at older pricing pages, the lineup has shifted. Here's where things stand now.

## Wrike Pricing Overview (2026)

| Plan | Price | Best For |
|------|-------|----------|
| Free | $0 | Individuals or teams of up to 5 |
| Team | $10/user/mo | Small teams needing shared task management |
| Business | $25/user/mo | Mid-size teams with cross-functional workflows |
| Pinnacle | ~$50/user/mo (custom) | Large teams needing advanced analytics |
| Apex | ~$80/user/mo (custom) | Enterprise-scale portfolio management |

All paid plans are billed annually. Monthly billing is available on Team and Business but adds roughly 20%. Pinnacle and Apex require annual contracts with custom quotes.

## Free Plan

Wrike's Free plan supports up to **5 users** and includes:

- **Board, table, and list views**
- **Task management** with subtasks
- **2GB storage** total
- **Cloud storage integrations** (Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.)
- **Web, desktop, and mobile apps**

It's a reasonable starting point for freelancers or a very small team testing the platform. But with a 5-user cap and no Gantt charts or automations, you'll outgrow it quickly if you're managing real projects.

## Team Plan — $10/user/month

The Team plan removes the user cap and adds the features most small teams actually need:

- **Unlimited users** (no minimum seat count)
- **Gantt charts** and interactive timeline view
- **Custom workflows** and request forms
- **50 automations per month**
- **2GB storage per user**

At $10/user/month, Wrike Team sits right between [ClickUp](/tools/clickup/) Unlimited ($7) and [Monday.com](/tools/monday/) Standard ($12). The trade-off: Wrike's interface has a steeper learning curve, but it handles complex task dependencies better than most tools at this price.

## Business Plan — $25/user/month

Business is Wrike's most popular tier and where the platform really differentiates itself. It requires a **minimum of 5 seats** (so $125/month minimum). You get:

- **Custom fields and workflows** (unlimited)
- **Real-time reports and dashboards**
- **Resource management** (workload view)
- **Project blueprints** for repeatable processes
- **Branded request forms** with conditional logic
- **200 automations per month**
- **5GB storage per user**
- **Salesforce, Adobe, and Tableau integrations**

If your team has more than 10 people or manages projects across departments, Business is likely the right pick. The resource management and reporting features alone justify the jump from Team.

## Pinnacle Plan — Custom Pricing (~$50/user/month)

Pinnacle is built for organizations that need advanced analytics and security:

- **Advanced analytics** with BI-level dashboards
- **Job roles and skill-based scheduling**
- **Locked spaces** for confidential projects
- **SAM and audit reports**
- **1,500 automations per month**
- **10GB storage per user**
- **SAML-based SSO**

Wrike doesn't publish a fixed Pinnacle price. Based on publicly shared quotes, expect roughly $50/user/month for teams of 50 or more, though volume discounts are common.

## Apex Plan — Custom Pricing (~$80/user/month)

New in January 2026, Apex replaces the old Enterprise tier and adds portfolio-level capabilities:

- **Everything in Pinnacle**, plus:
- **Advanced portfolio management**
- **Cross-organizational dashboards**
- **Custom API rate limits** (higher than standard)
- **Dedicated customer success manager**
- **99.9% uptime SLA**
- **Data residency controls** (US, EU, or AU)

Apex is clearly positioned for companies managing 100+ users across multiple business units. Pricing is fully custom — expect $80/user/month as a ballpark before enterprise discounts kick in.

## What Changed in 2026?

The biggest change: **Enterprise is gone.** Wrike consolidated its top tiers into Pinnacle and Apex. If you were on Enterprise, Wrike has been migrating those accounts to Pinnacle (with Apex as an upsell).

The Team and Business plans remain largely the same, though automation limits got a modest bump across the board.

Wrike's pricing is middle-of-the-road. ClickUp is cheaper and more generous with automations. Monday.com forces you into seat minimums earlier. [Asana](/tools/asana/) has a better free plan (10 users) but costs slightly more per seat on paid tiers.

Where Wrike wins is depth. The Business plan's resource management, project blueprints, and reporting are more mature than what you'll find in ClickUp or Monday at the same price point.

## Which Plan Should You Choose?

- **Solo or testing the platform**: Use the Free plan. It's limited to 5 users but gives you enough to evaluate the interface.
- **Small teams (5–15 people)**: The Team plan at $10/user/month covers the basics well — Gantt charts, custom workflows, and basic automations.
- **Mid-size teams (15–50 people)**: Business at $25/user/month is the sweet spot. Resource management and advanced reporting are essential at this scale.
- **Large organizations (50+ people)**: Talk to Wrike about Pinnacle or Apex. Pinnacle handles advanced analytics; Apex adds portfolio management and compliance controls.

## Bottom Line

Wrike's pricing is fair for a platform with this level of project management depth. The Team plan is competitive, and Business offers strong value for mid-size organizations. The Pinnacle/Apex split gives enterprise buyers clearer upgrade paths than the old Enterprise catch-all.

The main downside: Wrike's learning curve is steeper than simpler tools like Trello or Todoist. If your team needs something lighter, look at [ClickUp](/tools/clickup/) or [Asana](/tools/asana/) first.
