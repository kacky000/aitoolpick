Both Notion and Obsidian are popular with students, but they take fundamentally different approaches to [note-taking](/category/note-taking/). Notion is a cloud-first all-in-one workspace. Obsidian is a local-first Markdown editor with powerful linking. Which one should you use for school?

## Quick Verdict

**Choose Notion** if you want an organized, visual workspace for courses, tasks, and collaboration. **Choose Obsidian** if you want deep linking between concepts, offline access, and full control over your notes.

## The Fundamental Difference

**Notion** stores everything in the cloud. Your notes live on Notion's servers, accessible from any device with internet. It's a structured workspace with databases, pages, and embeds.

**Obsidian** stores everything locally as Markdown files on your computer. Your notes are plain text files you own forever. It uses bidirectional linking to connect ideas into a knowledge graph.

This core difference drives every other comparison point.

## Study System Comparison

### Taking Lecture Notes

**Notion**: Clean editor with toggle lists, callouts, and embedded media. Create a database of lectures sorted by course, date, and topic. Great for structured note-taking.

**Obsidian**: Markdown editor with quick formatting. Daily notes feature works well for lecture capture. Link concepts across lectures with `[[backlinks]]`. Better for stream-of-consciousness notes.

**Winner**: Notion for structured notes. Obsidian for speed and concept linking.

### Building a Knowledge Base

**Notion**: Organize by course with sub-pages. Create databases for terms, concepts, and formulas. Visual and browsable, but connections between courses are manual.

**Obsidian**: Bidirectional links create a web of connected concepts. The graph view visualizes how ideas relate across courses. Over a semester, your knowledge graph becomes a powerful study tool.

**Winner**: Obsidian — the graph view and backlinks create genuine knowledge connections that databases can't replicate.

### Research & Writing Papers

**Notion**: Good for outlining and drafting. Collect sources in databases. Collaborate with group project members. Export to Markdown for formatting elsewhere.

**Obsidian**: Excellent for research with the [Zotero](/tools/zotero/) plugin. Link sources to notes, build argument chains, and use the canvas feature for visual planning. Write in Markdown and export to any format via Pandoc.

**Winner**: Obsidian for solo research. Notion for group projects.

### Task & Assignment Tracking

**Notion**: Built-in databases with due dates, status, priority. Create a semester dashboard tracking all courses and assignments. Calendar and Kanban views included.

**Obsidian**: Possible with the Tasks plugin but requires setup. Not as intuitive as Notion's database approach. Most students use a separate tool for [task management](/category/task-management/).

**Winner**: Notion — task management is a core feature, not a plugin.

### Exam Preparation

**Notion**: Create study guides with toggle questions (click to reveal answers). Database of flashcards filtered by course. Share study materials with classmates.

**Obsidian**: Spaced repetition plugins turn notes into flashcards automatically. Graph view highlights weak connections in your understanding. Review mode helps identify gaps.

**Winner**: Tie. Different approaches, both effective.

## Pricing for Students

### Notion
- **Free plan**: Unlimited pages, limited file uploads
- **Education Plus**: Free for students with .edu email (includes unlimited file uploads, 30-day version history)
- No cost for most students

### Obsidian
- **Personal use**: Free forever
- **Sync** (optional): $4/month for cross-device sync
- **Publish** (optional): $8/month for publishing notes online
- Most students only need the free version + manual sync ([Google Drive](/tools/google-drive/), iCloud, etc.)

**Both are free for students** — cost isn't a deciding factor.

## Real Student Scenarios

### "I'm a first-year student setting up my system"
→ **Start with Notion**. Lower learning curve, built-in templates, and the Education plan is free. You can organize courses, track assignments, and take notes in one place.

### "I'm a STEM student with heavy math and research"
→ **Choose Obsidian**. Better LaTeX support through plugins, local file storage for research materials, and concept linking is invaluable for understanding interconnected topics.

### "I do lots of group projects"
→ **Choose Notion**. Real-time collaboration is built in. Share pages, assign tasks, and edit together.

### "I want my notes to last beyond college"
→ **Choose Obsidian**. Your notes are Markdown files on your computer — they'll be readable in 50 years. Notion's notes live on their servers and require export.

### "I switch between laptop, tablet, and phone"
→ **Choose Notion**. Seamless cloud sync included free. Obsidian Sync costs $4/month, or you can set up manual sync with a cloud folder.

## Can You Use Both?

Yes, and many students do:
- **Notion** for course organization, assignment tracking, and group projects
- **Obsidian** for deep note-taking, concept linking, and personal knowledge building

The downside is maintaining two systems. If you can only commit to one, pick based on your primary need.

## Bottom Line

**Notion** is the safer choice for most students — especially in the first two years when you need organization more than deep linking. It's easier to start, free with a student email, and handles tasks plus notes.

**Obsidian** rewards investment with a more powerful long-term knowledge system. If you're willing to spend time setting it up and learning plugins, it creates a personal knowledge base that compounds over your academic career.
