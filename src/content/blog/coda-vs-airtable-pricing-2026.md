**[Coda](/tools/coda/)** and **[Airtable](/tools/airtable/)** both blend documents and databases, but their pricing philosophies are opposite. Coda charges only for builders; Airtable charges every editor seat and gates you on records. The result is a big cost gap depending on how you work.

This guide breaks down both pricing models, the limits that actually trigger upgrades, and which tool is cheaper for your use case.

## The Key Difference: Makers vs Seats, Objects vs Records

Two different limits drive each tool's cost.

**Coda bills only "Makers."** Editors who input data and all viewers are free. Its constraint is **objects per doc** (1,000 on Free) — rows, columns, buttons, and formulas all count.

**Airtable bills every editor seat** and gates you on **records per base** (1,000 on Free, 50,000 on Team, 125,000 on Business). Read-only and commenter collaborators have limited free roles, but editors are paid.

So Coda's cost scales with the number of people *building*, while Airtable's scales with the number of people *editing* — and Airtable's per-seat price is double Coda's at the entry paid tier ($20 vs $10).

## Cost Scenario: A Team of 8 (3 Builders, 5 Editors)

- **Coda Pro**: 3 Makers × $10 = **$30/month** (5 editors free)
- **Airtable Team**: 8 seats × $20 = **$160/month**

That's a **5.3x difference**. The gap comes from both the per-seat price *and* the fact that Coda doesn't bill the 5 data-entry editors at all.

If all 8 people build equally:

- **Coda Pro**: 8 × $10 = **$80/month**
- **Airtable Team**: 8 × $20 = **$160/month**

Still **2x cheaper** on Coda, because Airtable's per-seat price is higher.

## Free Plan Comparison

- **Coda Free**: Unlimited docs/editors, **1,000 objects per doc**. Good for evaluation, tight for real trackers.
- **Airtable Free**: Up to 5 editors, **1,000 records per base**, limited automations. The record cap is the first wall most teams hit.

Both free plans are evaluation-grade. Airtable's record cap and 5-editor limit tend to force an upgrade faster for collaborative teams.

## Where Each One Wins on Value

- **Airtable** justifies its higher price with stronger native **database features**: views (Gantt, Kanban, calendar), interfaces, and a mature automation/sync layer. If you need a true relational database UI, the premium can be worth it.
- **Coda** wins on **cost efficiency and flexibility**: formula language, Packs, and the Maker model that keeps large teams cheap.

## Verdict

**Choose Coda** if cost matters and you have more consumers than builders. The Maker model plus the $10 Pro tier makes it the budget-friendly option by a wide margin.

**Choose Airtable** if you need serious database functionality — relational views, interfaces, and large-scale records — and you're willing to pay per seat for it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Coda cheaper than Airtable?

Almost always, yes. Coda's entry paid tier is $10/user vs Airtable's $20, and Coda only bills "Makers" while Airtable bills every editor seat.

### What's the record limit on Airtable's free plan?

Airtable Free allows 1,000 records per base and up to 5 editors. The Team plan raises this to 50,000 records per base.

### Does Coda limit how much data I can store?

Coda limits "objects" per doc (1,000 on Free). Pro and above remove the object cap, so storage is effectively unlimited for most use cases.

### Which is better for a relational database?

Airtable. Its relational model, views, and interfaces are more mature than Coda's table system, which is why it commands a higher price.
